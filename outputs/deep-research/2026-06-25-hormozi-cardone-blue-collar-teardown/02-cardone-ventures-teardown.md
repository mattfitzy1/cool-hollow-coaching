# Cardone Ventures Teardown: Offer Ladder, Funnel, and Method

Deep competitive teardown for Cool Hollow Coaching / Business Without You. Cardone Ventures is the closest live analog to our model: a finance-and-operations advisory firm that coaches owner-operators of small-to-mid businesses (heavily blue-collar: HVAC, plumbing, roofing, home services, construction, landscaping) to scale, fronted by two real operators (Grant Cardone, Brandon Dawson). This report reverse-engineers their offer ladder, their stranger-to-client funnel, Brandon Dawson's story and method, their content engine, who their clients actually are, and where they get criticized.

Date: 2026-06-25. Researcher: deep-research agent (web channel). Note on method limits at the end.

---

## Executive Summary

Cardone Ventures runs a textbook high-ticket ascension business with a blue-collar tilt. The shape is: free assessment/quiz/calculator at the top, cheap live workshops ($497 to $4,997) and a 2-day "10X360" implementation event in the middle, then a steep jump to high-ticket advisory: monthly group coaching at $2,499/mo, a six-month "Platform Review" reported at roughly $39,997 down plus $25,000/mo for 6 months (~$190K), and advisory/education "up to $250K per year." The engine that moves people up the ladder is a hard outbound sales floor: reps make 250-300 calls a day, book ~60 meetings a month, and are targeted to help close 5 "10X360s" and personally sell 10 Essentials workshop tickets to hit ~$200K/month in revenue, on uncapped commission.

The hook is identical to ours in structure but opposite in tone. They lead with a giant aspirational number ("add 30-50% to your bottom line in 12-18 months," "scale to $125M," "$0 to $100M net worth") fronted by Grant Cardone's billionaire brand and Brandon Dawson's genuinely credible $151M / 77x EBITDA exit. They go BIG, loud, and event-driven. The criticism that follows them is exactly the gap we should exploit: high-pressure sales at events, a $40,000 workshop a customer publicly called a scam ("did not provide the services I was sold"), and a "cultish," burnout sales culture in Glassdoor reviews.

For us: borrow the assessment-as-front-door, the vertical-specific events (10X HVAC, 10X Roofing), the named-operator credibility, and the "personal + professional + financial goals" framing. Avoid the hype number, the event-floor pressure close, and the vague "results not typical" promise. Our proof-led "$50K hidden profit found vs a $5K price, here's the receipt" is the antidote to the exact thing people resent about them.

## Key Findings

### 1. The offer ladder is free assessment → cheap live workshops → mid event → high-ticket advisory
Claim: Cardone Ventures runs a classic value-ladder, with free lead-capture tools, $497-$4,997 workshops/events, $2,499/mo group coaching, and high-ticket advisory in the low-to-mid six figures.
Evidence: Their products-and-services page lists free entry points (Business Breakpoint Quiz, Employee Profit Calculator, Business Scaling Formula Webinar, Home Services Playbook, 7 Elements Training), then Essentials Workshops, multi-day events (Elite Edge, Business Acquisition Summit), 10X360 (12-month implementation system for $1M+ businesses), the 10X360 Platform Review (6 months, "200+ page presentation"), and professional services (Financial, Marketing, Recruitment, Private Coaching, M&A). Group coaching is listed at $2,499/mo. Summit tickets are $497 / $1,997 / $4,997.
Confidence: High.
Triangulated sources: cardoneventures.com/products-and-services; 10x.cardoneventures.com/bootcamp; ba.cardoneventures.com/products/do-it-together-10x-group-coaching.

### 2. High-ticket pricing: ~$190K Platform Review, "up to $250K/year" advisory, $40K event reported
Claim: The real money sits in advisory engagements. Reported figures: Platform Review at $39,997 down + $25,000/mo x 6 months; advisory/education "up to $250K per year"; and at least one customer paid $40,000 for a 2-day "10X360" workshop.
Evidence: Web search surfaced a reported Platform Review structure of "$39,997 down payment and monthly payments are $25,000 per month for 6 months" (≈$189,997). Multiple sources state Cardone Ventures "charges up to $250K per year" for its education/advisory. A named customer (Rebecca Hamilton) publicly described paying "$40,000 for a 2-day workshop" sold at the 10X Growth Conference 2022.
Confidence: Medium-High on the $250K/yr and $40K figures (multiple independent mentions + a named first-person complaint); Medium on the exact Platform Review structure (single search synthesis, not seen on a primary page).
Triangulated sources: WebSearch results citing store.cardoneventures.com platform-review terms; scrapthesweettalk.podbean.com $40K complaint; multiple "up to $250K/year" mentions.

### 3. The stranger-to-client funnel is assessment → outbound sales floor → event → high-ticket close
Claim: Owners enter via a free quiz/calculator capturing revenue + phone, then a high-volume outbound sales team books them into a workshop/event, where senior closers ascend them into 10X360 / Platform Review / advisory.
Evidence: The Business Breakpoint Quiz is a 2-step form capturing name, email, annual revenue (tiers from under $250k to $100M+), and phone, with consent language authorizing email/text/phone follow-up; it explicitly targets owners earning $500k+ and is "a gateway to higher-ticket Cardone Ventures offerings like their 10X360 program." The Sales Representative job spec: "250-300 outbound calls daily," "up to 60 meetings a month," "assist toward the sale of 5 10X360s and personally close 10 Essentials workshop tickets to generate $200k in monthly revenue," comp $36K-$250K base range with top reps at "200-500k+," uncapped commission; duties include "calling cold leads, qualifying, and setting appointments with senior sales staff." The 10X360 event page captures the lead then routes to "a call with their Business Dev Team to map out a scaling blueprint" and build a "10X360 Business Plan" with a "90-day Quick Start Implementation Plan."
Confidence: High. The job posting is unusually explicit about the mechanics.
Triangulated sources: 10x.cardoneventures.com/control1691531290599 (quiz); Glassdoor/Teal/Salary.com sales-rep postings; 10x.cardoneventures.com/10x360.

### 4. Brandon Dawson's story is the credibility engine, and his method is "align personal + professional goals"
Claim: Dawson's real operator story (walnut farm → Sonus → fired by his own PE investors → Audigy → $151M / 77x EBITDA exit) is the spine of the brand's authority, and his method is aligning every employee's personal goals with the company's professional goals so growth compounds.
Evidence: Entrepreneur.com documents the arc: built Sonus to 1,000+ locations on $38M PE, rang the AMEX bell at 29, was removed as CEO when investors wanted to sell and he refused; founded Audigy in 2004 with no outside capital, gave nonvoting shares to loyal customers, grew to 650 practices / 1,800 employees / ~$300M aggregate revenue, sold 2016 for $151M (77x EBITDA) while staying CEO. His stated mission: "Help business owners win together with their teams and customers, without giving away too much of the business." His foundational lesson, from the walnut story: "Price isn't an issue if you create enough value." Cardone Ventures' recurring promise is to help owners hit their "personal, professional, and financial goals" via a "360-degree" view (operations, marketing, finance, people).
Confidence: High (third-party press + their own site, consistent).
Triangulated sources: entrepreneur.com/leadership/how-getting-fired...; howbrandsareborn.com origin story; cardoneventures.com (360 / goals language).

### 5. They are deeply blue-collar / trades focused, with vertical-specific events
Claim: Despite Grant Cardone's flashy brand, the actual client base skews blue-collar owner-operators, and they run trade-specific funnels.
Evidence: Dedicated verticals and events: 10X HVAC Summit, 10X Roofing Founders Event, 10X Home Services Boot Camp ("$500K to $5M or $5M to $50M," "Home Services industry"). Named case-study clients are overwhelmingly trades: General Air Conditioning & Plumbing, Malek Service Company (HVAC); Acker Bradbury Construction, Aquatica Pools and Spas, Savy & Sons (construction); Precision Landscaping and Irrigation, Lavender Landscape Design Co. (landscaping). A widely cited claim: took one HVAC client "from $20 million to $100 million in 18 months"; General Air "$11.2M to $15M revenue with 1138% growth in profit."
Confidence: High on the vertical focus; Medium on the specific outcome numbers (their own marketing, with a "results not typical / never a guarantee" disclaimer; single-source-ish).
Triangulated sources: cardoneventures.com/case-studies-business-growth; 10x.cardoneventures.com/hvacsummit, /homeservicesbootcamp, /10xroofing; globenewswire 10X HVAC Summit release.

### 6. The content engine = Grant's mega-brand + Brandon's "Building Billions" + scaling-tactic videos
Claim: Top-of-funnel is Grant Cardone's existing 10X audience (books, 10X events, social) fused with Brandon Dawson's own content: the "Building Billions" podcast, a YouTube channel of scaling/CEO-transition content, and a book (Nine Figure Mindset).
Evidence: Dawson's content stated mission: "help 1 MILLION business owners grow and scale." Formats observed: long-form interview/podcast appearances ("$0 to $150M," "How to Scale From a Business Owner to a Proper CEO," "The 3 M's of Scaling"), the Building Billions podcast, and books (Nine Figure Mindset by Dawson; TeamWork and Start The Work by Natalie Dawson; Emergency Business Response by Dawson). Grant Cardone supplies the 10X brand, 10X Growth Conference, and reach.
Confidence: High.
Triangulated sources: youtube.com Brandon Dawson channel + interview titles; bdawson.com; cardoneventures.com/products-and-services (books).

## Tensions & Contradictions (including complaints)

- "10X360" is an overloaded name. It is used for (a) a 2-day live event, (b) a 12-month implementation system, and (c) a 6-month "Platform Review." A buyer paid "$40,000 for a 2-day 10X360 workshop." This naming muddiness is itself a finding: it lets a cheap-sounding "event" share a name with a six-figure program, which is part of what the complaint resents.
- Promise vs. disclaimer. Marketing promises "add 30-50% to your bottom line in 12-18 months" and cites $20M→$100M in 18 months, but every case-study page carries "Examples given should not be considered typical and there is never a guarantee of results." The headline number and the fine print contradict each other. This is the credibility seam.
- Sales culture. Glassdoor reviews call it "cultish," cite outreach during PTO and bereavement, and say the floor "will bleed business owners dry" with "event after event." The same engine that drives their growth (250-300 calls/day, quota of 5 10X360s/mo) is what generates the over-selling complaints.
- The $40K scam complaint. Named customer, specific claim: paid $40K, "did not provide the services I was sold," company "refused to answer my generic questions" and "failed to show up to scheduled meetings on 2 separate occasions." Single-source but named and detailed. BBB shows only 3 formal complaints (A- rating), so this is not a flood, but the high price point makes each one loud.

## BORROW FOR BLUE-COLLAR

Concrete tactics to adapt for Business Without You (proof-led, not hype):

1. Assessment as the front door. Their Business Breakpoint Quiz ("97% of businesses don't make it past breakpoint 1 of 7") captures revenue + phone and frames the prospect's problem before any pitch. Our hidden-profit analyzer is the better version: an owner uploads a P&L, sees real leakage, and the receipt does the selling. Steal the "which of 7 stages are you stuck at" diagnostic framing for our quiz, mapped to our seven milestones.

2. Vertical-specific funnels and language. They don't say "business owners," they say "10X HVAC," "10X Roofing," "10X Home Services ($500K-$5M)." Our blue-collar niche should mirror this exactly: a named lane and event per trade rather than generic "small business." It makes the owner think "this is for me."

3. Named-operator credibility, told as a story arc. Dawson's "fired from my own company → built it again my way → $151M exit" is a narrative, not a brag. Mark has a real finance/advisory firm (Cool Hollow Solutions, ~35 clients, ~$60K/mo). Lead with his operator story the same way, as a story (consistent with our "video story arc" rule), not a slogan.

4. The "personal + professional + financial goals" frame. Their whole pitch hangs on aligning the owner's life with the business, not just revenue. This is on-brand for us: "15 hours back" is the personal goal, "$50K profit found" is the financial goal. We already have the better, more honest version of this.

5. A clear value-ladder with a cheap, low-risk first yes. They ascend free quiz → cheap workshop → recurring coaching → advisory. We have $5K Tier 1 → $22K/yr Tier 2; consider a genuinely cheap or free first rung (the analyzer + a paid mini-workshop or the milestone-1 "15 hours back" session) so the owner experiences a win before the $5K ask.

6. Five-pillar / 360 simplicity. Their "People, Marketing, Operations, Finance" wheel is memorable and sortable. Our seven milestones are stronger but should be packaged as a single visual the owner can see themselves on, the way the 10X360 wheel works.

7. Outcome-anchored pricing logic. Dawson's walnut lesson, "price isn't an issue if you create enough value," is literally our $50K-found-vs-$5K-price argument. Make the ROI math the centerpiece, but with a real number from their own P&L, not a borrowed case study.

8. Bundle the "after the program" services as the ascension path. Their back end is Financial/Marketing/Recruitment services and M&A. Our Tier 2 (CFO/COO/HR team, tax/investment/scaling) is the same shape. Position Tier 2 as the natural "now build the team" continuation of Tier 1's milestone 7.

### What to AVOID (where they get criticized, and our brand is the opposite)

- Don't lead with the giant aspirational number. "Add 30-50% to your bottom line," "$0 to $100M," "$20M to $100M in 18 months" all collide with their own "results not typical" fine print. That gap is exactly what fuels the "scam" complaint. We lead with a specific, defensible promise (15 hours back; up to $50K found) and we show the receipt.
- Don't run a pressure sales floor. 250-300 calls/day and a quota of "5 10X360s a month" produces the over-selling that customers and ex-employees resent ("will bleed business owners dry," "aggressive and misleading"). Our model is application + proof, not boiler-room volume. The analyzer pre-qualifies so we never have to push.
- Don't sell at the back of the room with an inflated event price. The named $40K-workshop complaint is the cautionary tale: high price + live-event urgency + thin deliverable = reputational damage. Our $5K Tier 1 with a concrete, listed deliverable (and Tier 2 clearly scoped) is the trust play.
- Don't overload program names. Their "10X360" means three different things at three prices; that ambiguity is part of why a buyer felt misled. Keep our names one-to-one with one thing and one price.
- Don't promise without proof or a guarantee posture. They hide behind "never a guarantee." We can do the opposite and make it a wedge: a clear scope, and the analyzer result as evidence the profit is actually there before the owner pays.

Net: Cardone Ventures has proven the demand (blue-collar owners will pay five and six figures to get out of the day-to-day and scale) and proven the funnel (assessment → vertical event → high-ticket advisory). They have also proven the resentment that hype + pressure creates. Our entire edge is being the proof-led, no-pressure, receipt-first version of the exact same offer.
